Concurrent Atomistic-Continuum Method

Back to Multiscale Methods

Introduction

Most partitioned-domain methods treat the continuum domain such that lattice dislocations are either implemented via elastic constitutive relations, or not permitted at all. This motivated development of a new multiscale method called the concurrent atomistic-continuum (CAC) method that admits displacement discontinuities between elements in the continuum domain. Within each element, lattice defects are not allowed and the displacement field has C1 continuity. Between elements, however, neither displacement continuity nor strain compatibility is required.

The theoretical foundation of the CAC method is the atomistic field theory (AFT). It is rooted in micromorphic theory, where a local density function is used to connect the micromorphic theory and molecular dynamics, in which a crystalline material is viewed as a continuous collection of lattice points, while embedded within each point is a unit cell with a group of discrete atoms (Chen, 2009). The same balance equations for both fully resolved atomistic and coarse-grained continuum domains are employed to compute the properties of general crystals. In the continuum domain, the interatomic potential serves as the only constitutive relation.

History

2003
Proposed AFT, laid the theoretical foundation of the CAC methods.
2004
Began the implementation of the CAC code at George Washington University.
2009
Employ the discontinous elements to accomondate defects in the continuum domain.
2015
Proposed a quasistatic implementation of CAC (Xu et al., 2015).

Features

  1. Use rhombohedral elements whose surfaces correspond to slip planes in FCC or BCC lattices.
  2. Accommodate planar dislocations and intrinsic stacking faults in the continuum domain.
  3. No need of ad hoc constitutive relations.
  4. Coarse-graining efficiency varies from 50 to 150, depending on element sizes.

Applications

  1. Dislocation nucleation from notched specimen
  2. Dislocation-void interaction
  3. Brittle and ductile fracture
  4. Dislocation-grain boundary interaction
